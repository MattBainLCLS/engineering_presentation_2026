Hollow Core Fibre Architectures

Solid-Core Fibre

  • Light guided by Total Internal Reflection (TIR) — requires n_core > n_cladding (~1.45 silica vs ~1.44 doped cladding).
  • Light travels entirely through glass: subject to full material dispersion and Kerr nonlinearity.
  • The reference point — everything hollow-core is measured against this.

Kagome HC-PCF

  • Guidance via Inhibited Coupling (IC): the kagome web geometry suppresses spatial overlap between the hollow-core mode and modes of the silica struts, preventing energy transfer rather than reflecting it.
  • Very broad transmission bandwidth (can span an octave), but loss is higher than anti-resonant designs (~dB/m range).
  • Name comes from the Japanese kagome basket-weave pattern — the silica strut topology directly echoes this.
  • Good choice when bandwidth matters more than loss, e.g. few-cycle pulse delivery.

Anti-Resonant HCF

  • Guidance via anti-resonant reflection from thin capillary walls: wall thickness t chosen so the operating wavelength sits between Fabry–Pérot resonances, maximising reflectivity back into the core.
  • Modern designs (NANF — Nested Anti-Resonant Nodeless Fibre) achieve loss <0.1 dB/km, approaching conventional fibre.
  • No contact points between adjacent capillaries ("nodeless") reduces scattering loss at the nodes.
  • This is the architecture used in this work.

Hollow Capillary

  • Simplest design — a plain glass tube. No photonic structuring.
  • Not truly a guided mode: the EH₁₁ mode leaks, with loss ∝ (λ/a)³ — favouring large core radius a and long wavelength.
  • Historically the first HCF used for nonlinear optics (Marcatili & Schmeltzer, 1964); still used for high-energy pulse delivery where loss is acceptable.
  • At LCLS: large-bore capillaries used to transport mJ-level pulses over short distances.

Key Comparison Point

  • In all hollow-core designs, light propagates through air/gas: nonlinearity reduced ~1000×, material dispersion ~1000× lower than glass.
  • Trade-off landscape: solid core (low loss, high nonlinearity) → capillary (leaky, broadband) → kagome (guided, very broadband) → anti-resonant (low loss, moderate bandwidth).

Fiber Types

Solid Core Fiber

  • Core: solid silica glass (n ≈ 1.45)
  • Cladding: doped silica, slightly lower n
  • Guidance via Total Internal Reflection (TIR)
  • Requires ncore > ncladding
  • Light travels inside glass — subject to material dispersion and nonlinearity

Hollow Core Fiber

  • Core: air or vacuum (n ≈ 1.00)
  • Cladding: microstructured silica (photonic crystal or anti-resonant rings)
  • TIR is not possible — ncore < ncladding
  • Guidance via Photonic Bandgap or Anti-Resonant Reflection
  • Light travels through air — dramatically reduced dispersion and nonlinearity

The inversion of refractive index contrast is the key distinction: hollow core guidance requires structural engineering of the cladding rather than a simple step-index boundary.

Pulse Compression

The Problem

  • Propagation through any dispersive medium stretches pulses in time
  • Glass optics introduce positive group delay dispersion (GDD) — red frequencies arrive before blue
  • Longer pulse duration reduces peak intensity, limiting nonlinear processes and damage thresholds
  • Goal: achieve transform-limited (shortest possible) pulses at the target

Two-Stage HCF Compression

  • Stage 1 — Spectral broadening: pulse propagates through gas-filled HCF; SPM creates new frequency components, broadening the spectrum
  • Stage 2 — Dispersion compensation: chirped mirrors or grating pairs apply negative GDD, realigning frequency components in time
  • Result: pulse duration compressed by 10–100×, approaching the few-cycle regime (< 10 fs)
  • HCF enables high-energy inputs (> mJ) without damage — impossible in solid-core fiber
