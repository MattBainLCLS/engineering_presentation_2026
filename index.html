Motivation — Why Ultrafast Pulses at LCLS?

The Science Case

  • Many physical and chemical processes (bond breaking, electron dynamics, phase transitions) occur on femtosecond timescales (10⁻¹⁵ s)
  • LCLS X-ray free-electron laser produces ~10 fs X-ray pulses — optical lasers must match this to enable pump–probe experiments
  • Shorter pulses → higher time resolution → ability to capture atomic motion in real time
  • Higher peak intensity → access to strong-field and nonlinear regimes at lower pulse energies

The Engineering Problem

  • Standard laser oscillators and amplifiers produce pulses of ~100 fs to several ps — not short enough
  • Conventional optics (lenses, windows, fibres) stretch pulses through dispersion and nonlinear phase accumulation
  • High-energy pulses (>mJ) damage solid-core fibres before useful nonlinear effects occur
  • Hollow core fibres provide a path to compress pulses while handling high energies without material damage
  • LCLS operates pump–probe experiments where an optical laser excites a sample and X-rays probe the structural response. Temporal resolution is limited by whichever pulse is longer — so optical pulses must be as short as the X-ray pulses (~10 fs).
  • The key engineering challenge: getting from ~200 fs (typical Ti:Sapphire amplifier output) down to <10 fs without destroying the optics or beam quality.
  • Hollow core fibres solve the damage threshold problem by keeping the light in air/gas rather than glass.

What is an Ultrafast Laser Pulse?

Time–Bandwidth Product

  • A pulse of duration Δt must have a minimum spectral bandwidth Δν: Δt · Δν ≥ 0.44 (Gaussian pulse)
  • A 10 fs pulse requires >44 THz bandwidth — spanning a significant fraction of the visible/NIR spectrum
  • Transform-limited (TL) pulse: all frequency components arrive in phase → shortest possible duration for a given spectrum
  • Any spectral phase distortion (dispersion, nonlinearity) stretches the pulse beyond the TL duration

Key Parameters

  • Pulse duration: typically measured as FWHM of temporal intensity envelope (fs–ps)
  • Peak power: P₀ = E_pulse / (√π · τ_FWHM) — can reach GW–TW for mJ pulses at 10–100 fs
  • Carrier frequency: 800 nm (Ti:Sapphire) or 1030 nm (Yb:KGW) are common pump wavelengths at LCLS
  • Spectral phase: φ(ω) — the phase relationship between frequency components; flat φ → transform-limited pulse
  • The time–bandwidth product is the fundamental reason you need broad bandwidth to make short pulses. A narrowband CW laser cannot be made into a short pulse regardless of how you modulate it.
  • Peak power is the relevant quantity for nonlinear optics — 1 mJ in 30 fs gives ~30 GW. Same energy in 1 ns gives only 1 MW. The peak power determines whether you cross the threshold for SPM, soliton formation, etc.
  • Ti:Sapphire at 800 nm is the dominant system at LCLS for historical reasons and its broad gain bandwidth (supports <5 fs pulses). Yb-based systems are emerging for higher repetition rates.

Fiber Types

Solid Core Fiber

  • Core: solid silica glass (n ≈ 1.45)
  • Cladding: doped silica, slightly lower n
  • Guidance via Total Internal Reflection (TIR)
  • Requires ncore > ncladding
  • Light travels inside glass — subject to material dispersion and nonlinearity

Hollow Core Fiber

  • Core: air or vacuum (n ≈ 1.00)
  • Cladding: microstructured silica (photonic crystal or anti-resonant rings)
  • TIR is not possible — ncore < ncladding
  • Guidance via Photonic Bandgap or Anti-Resonant Reflection
  • Light travels through air — dramatically reduced dispersion and nonlinearity

The inversion of refractive index contrast is the key distinction: hollow core guidance requires structural engineering of the cladding rather than a simple step-index boundary.

Hollow Core Fibre Architectures

Solid-Core Fibre

  • Light guided by Total Internal Reflection (TIR) — requires n_core > n_cladding (~1.45 silica vs ~1.44 doped cladding).
  • Light travels entirely through glass: subject to full material dispersion and Kerr nonlinearity.
  • The reference point — everything hollow-core is measured against this.

Kagome HC-PCF

  • Guidance via Inhibited Coupling (IC): the kagome web geometry suppresses spatial overlap between the hollow-core mode and modes of the silica struts, preventing energy transfer rather than reflecting it.
  • Very broad transmission bandwidth (can span an octave), but loss is higher than anti-resonant designs (~dB/m range).
  • Name comes from the Japanese kagome basket-weave pattern — the silica strut topology directly echoes this.
  • Good choice when bandwidth matters more than loss, e.g. few-cycle pulse delivery.

Anti-Resonant HCF

  • Guidance via anti-resonant reflection from thin capillary walls: wall thickness t chosen so the operating wavelength sits between Fabry–Pérot resonances, maximising reflectivity back into the core.
  • Modern designs (NANF — Nested Anti-Resonant Nodeless Fibre) achieve loss <0.1 dB/km, approaching conventional fibre.
  • No contact points between adjacent capillaries ("nodeless") reduces scattering loss at the nodes.
  • This is the architecture used in this work.

Hollow Capillary

  • Simplest design — a plain glass tube. No photonic structuring.
  • Not truly a guided mode: the EH₁₁ mode leaks, with loss ∝ (λ/a)³ — favouring large core radius a and long wavelength.
  • Historically the first HCF used for nonlinear optics (Marcatili & Schmeltzer, 1964); still used for high-energy pulse delivery where loss is acceptable.
  • At LCLS: large-bore capillaries used to transport mJ-level pulses over short distances.

Key Comparison Point

  • In all hollow-core designs, light propagates through air/gas: nonlinearity reduced ~1000×, material dispersion ~1000× lower than glass.
  • Trade-off landscape: solid core (low loss, high nonlinearity) → capillary (leaky, broadband) → kagome (guided, very broadband) → anti-resonant (low loss, moderate bandwidth).

Self-Phase Modulation — Spectral Broadening

Pulse Compression

The Problem

  • Propagation through any dispersive medium stretches pulses in time
  • Glass optics introduce positive group delay dispersion (GDD) — red frequencies arrive before blue
  • Longer pulse duration reduces peak intensity, limiting nonlinear processes and damage thresholds
  • Goal: achieve transform-limited (shortest possible) pulses at the target

Two-Stage HCF Compression

  • Stage 1 — Spectral broadening: pulse propagates through gas-filled HCF; SPM creates new frequency components, broadening the spectrum
  • Stage 2 — Dispersion compensation: chirped mirrors or grating pairs apply negative GDD, realigning frequency components in time
  • Result: pulse duration compressed by 10–100×, approaching the few-cycle regime (< 10 fs)
  • HCF enables high-energy inputs (> mJ) without damage — impossible in solid-core fiber

The spectral phase imprinted by SPM (see previous slide) is the bandwidth resource that dispersion compensation converts into temporal compression.

Spectral Phase — Taylor Expansion

$$\varphi(\omega) = \varphi_0 + \underbrace{\left.\frac{d\varphi}{d\omega}\right|_{\omega_0}}_{\mathrm{GD}} \!(\omega - \omega_0) + \frac{1}{2}\underbrace{\left.\frac{d^2\varphi}{d\omega^2}\right|_{\omega_0}}_{\mathrm{GDD}} \!(\omega - \omega_0)^2 + \frac{1}{6}\underbrace{\left.\frac{d^3\varphi}{d\omega^3}\right|_{\omega_0}}_{\mathrm{TOD}} \!(\omega - \omega_0)^3 + \cdots$$

$\varphi_0$ — Carrier-Envelope Phase

  • Absolute phase offset between the carrier wave and the pulse envelope
  • Shifts all frequencies equally — no effect on pulse shape or duration
  • Critical only for few-cycle pulses where CEP changes the peak electric field

GD — Group Delay

  • Linear phase term: shifts the pulse envelope in time without distortion
  • Units: fs (or s) — the arrival time of the pulse centre
  • Irrelevant to pulse duration; sets the transit time through a medium

GDD — Group Delay Dispersion

  • Quadratic phase: different frequencies travel at different group velocities
  • Units: fs² — positive GDD stretches the pulse (red before blue in silica)
  • Dominant term in most optical systems; compensated by chirped mirrors or gratings

TOD — Third-Order Dispersion

  • Cubic phase: creates asymmetric satellite pulses and a pedestal in time
  • Units: fs³ — becomes significant for broadband (<30 fs) pulses
  • Residual TOD after GDD compensation is often the limit on compressed pulse quality
  • The Taylor expansion is the universal language of pulse propagation — every dispersive element (glass, fibre, air) can be characterised by its GDD and TOD coefficients.
  • For silica glass at 800 nm: GDD ≈ +36 fs²/mm, TOD ≈ +27 fs³/mm. A 1 mm window already adds enough GDD to stretch a 30 fs pulse to ~40 fs.
  • The next slide lets you dial in GDD and TOD interactively and see the effect on both the spectral phase and temporal pulse shape.
  • Note that SPM adds a nonlinear phase φ_NL(t) = γ P(t) L in the time domain — not a simple polynomial in ω. It broadens the spectrum but the resulting spectral phase is not simply GDD or TOD.

Dispersion — GDD & TOD on an Ultrafast Pulse

Soliton Self-Compression

What is a Soliton?

  • A soliton is a pulse that propagates without changing shape — dispersion and nonlinearity exactly balance
  • In anomalous dispersion (β₂ < 0), negative GDD competes with SPM-induced positive chirp
  • A higher-order soliton (soliton number N > 1) undergoes periodic compression and splitting — it first compresses dramatically before dispersing
  • Soliton number: N² = L_D / L_NL = γ P₀ τ² / |β₂| — set by pulse energy and fiber parameters

HCF Advantage

  • Anti-resonant HCF can be designed with anomalous dispersion at 800 nm by controlling core size and gas pressure
  • Low nonlinearity (air vs glass) allows high pulse energies while maintaining the correct N for soliton compression
  • Compression ratio scales as N — typical values N = 5–15 give 5–15× temporal compression in a single pass
  • Unlike chirped-mirror compression, soliton compression requires no additional optics after the fibre
  • The key insight is that in anomalous dispersion, GDD and SPM have opposite signs of chirp — GDD stretches the pulse in time (red before blue for anomalous) while SPM creates new blue frequencies at the leading edge and red at the trailing edge. At the right power level (N=1), these exactly cancel. For N>1, the nonlinearity dominates early and the pulse compresses first.
  • In hollow core fibre, you get anomalous dispersion "for free" at the right core size — unlike solid silica which has normal dispersion below 1.3 µm. This is why HCF enables soliton compression at 800 nm.
  • The dispersive wave emission on the next slide is a direct consequence of higher-order soliton dynamics — energy shed by the soliton into a phase-matched resonance.

Conclusions & Outlook

What We've Shown

  • Anti-resonant hollow core fibres enable high-energy ultrafast pulse compression through SPM-based spectral broadening and soliton dynamics
  • Soliton self-compression in HCF produces few-cycle pulses (<10 fs) from ~200 fs amplifier outputs in a single fibre stage
  • Resonant dispersive wave emission generates tunable deep-UV light (sub-250 nm), capturing 22% of the total pulse energy
  • HCF-based UV sources are well-matched to the LCLS pump–probe experimental programme

Next Steps

  • Characterise compressed pulse duration via FROG or d-scan measurement at the fibre output
  • Optimise gas type and pressure to tune RDW emission wavelength for specific experimental targets
  • Demonstrate coupling of the UV output into the LCLS pump–probe beamline
  • Explore nested anti-resonant nodeless fibre (NANF) geometries for reduced propagation loss at longer fibre lengths
  • The overall story arc: we need short pulses at LCLS → conventional fibres can't handle the energy → HCF solves both the damage and dispersion problems → soliton dynamics give us efficient compression → dispersive waves give us tunable UV for science.
  • The characterisation step (FROG/d-scan) is essential — simulation shows good compression but the real output needs to be verified, especially the spectral phase.
  • Gas pressure tuning is a practical knob: higher pressure increases nonlinearity (more SPM, shorter RDW wavelength) and changes the dispersion landscape.
